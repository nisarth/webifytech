## Strategy 1: Architecting for Crawlability and Indexing
For e-commerce sites, technical debt is the silent killer of rankings. With hundreds or thousands of products, faceted navigation (filters) can create millions of duplicate URLs that waste your "crawl budget."

### The Technical Fixes
- **Smart Faceted Navigation**: Use canonical tags and robots.txt to prevent search engines from indexing low-value filter combinations (e.g., size + color + price range).
- **Core Web Vitals for E-Commerce**: In 2025, page speed is a non-negotiable ranking factor. Every millisecond of delay in your [LINK: technical-seo-audit-2025] is a potential lost sale.
- **XML Sitemap Automation**: Ensure your sitemap updates in real-time as products are added or removed.

## FAQ

### How long does e-commerce SEO take to work?
Typically, you will see technical improvements within 2-4 weeks. Significant organic traffic growth usually takes 3-6 months of consistent strategy. However, once established, organic traffic provides the highest ROI of any marketing channel.

### Should I delete out-of-stock products?
Generally, no. If the product is temporary out of stock, keep the page and offer "Notify Me" or "Related Products." If it is permanently discontinued, use a 301 redirect to the most relevant category or a newer version of the product to preserve its link juice. Learn more in our [LINK: content-auditing-for-seo].

### Is Shopify really bad for SEO?
No. Shopify is excellent for SEO out of the box. However, it does have some rigidities in URL structure and faceted navigation. For brands requiring total control over their technical SEO, we often explore [LINK: headless-commerce-vs-shopify-plus].

### How do I optimize for voice search in e-commerce?
Focus on conversational, "long-tail" keywords that mimic how people speak. "Where can I buy [product] near me?" or "Best [category] for [use-case]." Use FAQ schema to provide direct answers. See our [LINK: voice-search-optimization-2025] for more.

### Should I prioritize product pages or category pages?
Category pages often have higher search volume for "broad" terms (e.g., "men's sneakers"), while product pages target "high-intent" specific terms (e.g., "brand X model y sneakers size 10"). A healthy strategy targets both by using category pages as content hubs that link to individual products.
